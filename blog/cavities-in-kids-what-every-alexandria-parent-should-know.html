Cavities in Kids: What Every Alexandria Parent Should Know

By Dr. Hye Park, DMD · Published June 21, 2025

Cavities are one of the most common chronic conditions in children, but they're also one of the most preventable. If you're a parent in Alexandria, you're not alone in wondering how to best protect your child's smile. At Green Dentistry, we see firsthand how cavities can sneak up on even the most health-conscious families. Fortunately, with a little knowledge and consistent dental care, you can dramatically reduce your child's risk of tooth decay.

Let's break down everything parents should know about cavities in kids, from what causes them to how you can prevent them, with support from your trusted local pediatric dentist.

What Exactly Is a Cavity?

A cavity, or dental caries, is a small hole or area of decay that forms when tooth enamel is damaged. This damage is caused by acids produced when bacteria in the mouth feed on sugars and carbohydrates left behind on the teeth.

The enamel on baby teeth is thinner and softer than on adult teeth, which makes children especially vulnerable. Once a cavity forms, it won't go away on its own, it requires professional dental treatment to restore the tooth.

Why Are Children More Susceptible to Cavities?

There are a few reasons why children are particularly at risk for cavities:

  • Developing brushing habits: Young kids often need supervision and help brushing properly and reaching all areas of the mouth.
  • High-sugar diets: Juice, fruit snacks, sticky candies, and even some cereals are high in sugar that can stick to the teeth.
  • Thinner enamel: As mentioned, baby teeth don't have the same strength or structure as adult teeth.
  • Frequent snacking: Grazing throughout the day without brushing afterward increases the risk of acid buildup.

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), more than 50% of children aged 6 to 8 have had at least one cavity in their baby teeth.

Signs Your Child Might Have a Cavity

Cavities don't always cause obvious pain right away, especially in the early stages. Still, there are signs you can look for, including:

  • Tooth sensitivity: Complaints about discomfort with cold, hot, or sweet foods
  • Visible holes or spots: White, brown, or black areas on the teeth
  • Toothache: Ongoing pain in a specific tooth
  • Avoidance of chewing on one side: This could mean discomfort or pressure sensitivity

If you notice any of these signs, it's a good idea to schedule a checkup at Green Dentistry for an evaluation.

Prevention Tips for Parents in Alexandria

Daily habits make a big difference when it comes to your child's oral health. Here are the most effective ways to reduce their risk of cavities:

  • Brush and floss regularly: Kids should brush twice a day for two minutes and floss daily.
  • Monitor brushing technique: For children under age 7, parents should assist or supervise brushing to ensure thorough cleaning.
  • Limit sugary snacks and drinks: Reserve treats for mealtimes and avoid sugary drinks like soda or juice between meals.
  • Drink water: Encourage tap water, especially after meals, to rinse away sugars and acids.
  • Schedule dental visits: Routine checkups every six months allow early detection and professional cleanings.

What Happens If a Child Gets a Cavity?

If your child develops a cavity, the most common treatment is a tooth-colored filling, also known as a composite restoration. In some cases where the cavity is deeper, your dentist may recommend a pulpotomy (a type of baby tooth root treatment) or a dental crown to protect the tooth from further damage.

Dr. Hye Park and the team at Green Dentistry create a calm, kid-friendly environment so young patients feel safe and supported during treatment. The earlier we catch the cavity, the simpler and more comfortable the solution will be.

What If My Child Has Special Needs or Anxiety?

We understand that not every child feels comfortable in the dental chair. Our team is trained in pediatric behavior techniques and offers a compassionate, gentle approach tailored to each child. For anxious children or those with special needs, we may recommend nitrous oxide (laughing gas) or other comfort options to make the experience smoother.

What the Experts Say

The American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry (AAPD) recommends that children visit a dentist by their first birthday or within six months of their first tooth eruption. Early dental visits not only help catch decay in its early stages, they also establish a dental home and build comfort with the dentist.

Common Parent Myths About Cavities in Kids

Let's clear up a few common misconceptions:

  • "They're just baby teeth, they'll fall out anyway." Yes, baby teeth fall out, but decay can lead to pain, infection, and damage to the developing adult teeth underneath.
  • "If my child doesn't eat candy, they won't get cavities." Many foods, including crackers, granola bars, and even fruit juice, contain sugars that can fuel bacteria.
  • "My child brushes every day, so we're in the clear." Brushing is crucial, but technique and consistency matter, and flossing is essential, too.
